Subtheme: Exclusionary Zoning

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How does changing the zoning of a community affect the demographics of the people living there? Will there be mass migration of a certain group if zoning laws changed and another group of people moved in? What are these groups? What are the implications of the new demographic landscape of the community on schools, jobs, public policy, etc?
    2.
    • Motivation: In "Living Little", one of the options for increasing housing supply is single family home conversions. In this case, people living in single family homes will have neighbors living in small housing. These groups of people will most likely have different demographics. In the hisotrical past and from my own lived experiences, when one demographic moves into an area, another demographic moves out such as during gentrification of a community or white flight. One such example in Boston is the Great Migration where Black southerners moved northwards.
  2. Are there any geographical patterns to the zoning classifications today that we can attribute to events/themes in history? Do certain areas have more single family zoning than others because of what that area was used for or close to in the past? What exclusionary themes do these patterns imply?
    3.
    • Motivation: Boston is a very old city and has gone through a lot of social and geographical changes. Exclusionary by Design argues zoning was used to exclude groups, especially during the Big Downzone in the 1970s.I wonder if the effects are still relevant today and if so, then how far into the future do we need to think about when designing public policies?
  3. Does having a single family zoning area affect the zoning of the nearby areas? What types of zoning surround a single family zoning area? How large is the difference in socioeconomic status/race between the two sides of the boundary?
    4.
    • Motivation: Exclusionary By Design argues that zoning is by definition a form of legal separation. However, to have separation, there must be a boundary and there must be something on the other side of the boundary that is different. I've heard a lot about the "rich side of town" and the "poor side of town" from many of my friends and wonder how that applies to zoning.
  4. Does public policy force necessary upzoning changes on a community or cause further restrictions and segregation as loopholes are found?
    5.
    • Motivation: Exclusionary by Design talks about the MBTA Communities Upzoning law that mandates areas served by the MBTA zone a reasonable area for multifamily housing. However, are areas really being upzoned or will new lines just be built in areas that are already upzoned, making it even harder for marginalized communities to have reliable transit to where they need to go? In high school, I remember learning about the congressional district lines in California being gerrymandered so that Beverly Hills is in a wealthier district overall even though all the cities around it are in a different district. In this, public policy causes further segregation.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Without any temporal data in this dataset, it was difficult to answer many of my initial questions relating to causation and history. However, from a mix of Google, Figure 1, and Figures 6-8, it is evident that exclusionary zoning is present, especially during the Big Downzone of the 1970s and 80s that targeted Black and Hispanic/Latino communities for downzoning. There also seems to be a correlation between median income of a municipality and the percentage of single family zones, although it is not always a strong correlation. Additionally, the MBTA Rapid Transit communities are the only communities so far that have been required to upzone so there are still quite a few municipalities with 100% single family zones. A more thorough and deeper analysis could be done if the dataset included temporal data, more granular income data, and recent zoning changes and if I knew more about zoning, particularly in the Metro Boston area.
